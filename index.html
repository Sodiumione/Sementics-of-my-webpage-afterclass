QURAN

The Quran, the foundational text of Islam, is revered by Muslims as the direct and unaltered word of God, revealed to the Prophet Muhammad through the angel Gabriel over a period of approximately 23 years. This divine revelation is captured in classical Arabic and is composed of 114 surahs (chapters) that vary greatly in length, each containing numerous verses (ayahs) that address a broad spectrum of topics including theology, morality, law, and guidance for personal and communal conduct. The Quran is not only a spiritual guide but also a comprehensive framework for living a righteous life, encompassing aspects of faith, worship, ethics, and social justice. It provides detailed instructions on how to interact with others, handle various life situations, and maintain justice and compassion in society. For Muslims, the Quran is recited and memorized during daily prayers and throughout life, serving as a constant source of inspiration and reflection. Its profound impact extends beyond religious practice, influencing Islamic art, literature, and legal systems, and shaping cultural and social norms across diverse Muslim societies. The Quran’s teachings continue to resonate with millions, offering timeless wisdom and guidance that shape the spiritual and moral dimensions of their lives.

TAWRAT

In Islam, the Tawrat is regarded as one of the major divine revelations, given by God to the Prophet Moses (Musa) as a guiding scripture for the Israelites. This sacred text, which comprises the first five books of the Hebrew Bible—Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, and Deuteronomy—contains comprehensive laws, commandments, and ethical teachings intended to direct the moral and social conduct of its followers. Muslims acknowledge the Tawrat as a significant part of the continuum of divine guidance, respecting its role in shaping the religious and cultural identity of the Jewish people. However, Islamic tradition also holds that while the Tawrat was originally a true and uncorrupted revelation, it has undergone changes and distortions over time. This belief is based on the Islamic view that previous scriptures, including the Tawrat, were altered or misinterpreted before the final revelation, the Quran, was sent to Prophet Muhammad. The Quran, which Muslims believe to be the ultimate and unaltered word of God, is seen as both confirming the truth of earlier scriptures and providing necessary corrections to any deviations that may have occurred. In this way, the Tawrat is honored as part of a broader divine message, while the Quran is viewed as the final and complete guidance for humanity.

ZABUR

In Islam, the Zabur is regarded as one of the sacred scriptures revealed by Allah to Prophet Dawood (David). The Quran acknowledges the Zabur in several verses, affirming its divine origin and significance. While the exact contents of the Zabur are not detailed in the Quran, it is understood to consist of poetic hymns and psalms that emphasize themes of divine worship, morality, and righteousness. The Quranic references highlight the Zabur's role in the broader context of divine revelation, which includes the Torah (Tawrat) given to Prophet Musa (Moses) and the Injil (Gospel) given to Prophet Isa (Jesus). These scriptures, including the Zabur, are seen as part of a continuum of guidance provided by Allah to humanity through various prophets. Although the Zabur itself is not extensively detailed in Islamic texts beyond these references, it is respected for its contribution to the prophetic tradition and its role in reinforcing the core message of monotheism and ethical conduct shared across the Abrahamic faiths. Thus, the Zabur occupies an important place in Islamic spirituality and theology, reflecting the continuity and consistency of divine guidance through history.

INJIL

In Islam, the Injil is recognized as a sacred scripture revealed by Allah to Prophet Isa (Jesus), and it is considered an integral part of the divine message sent to humanity. The Injil, often equated with the Gospel in Christian tradition, is believed to have contained guidance, teachings, and moral principles intended for Jesus’ followers, promoting righteousness and ethical conduct. The Quran acknowledges the Injil in various verses, such as in Surah Al-Imran (3:3-4), which states that Allah sent down the Torah, the Injil, and the Criterion (Quran) as guidance for people. Despite its revered status, Islam holds that the Injil, like previous scriptures, has undergone alterations and misinterpretations over time. As a result, while the Injil is respected as part of the prophetic tradition, the Quran is regarded as the final and complete revelation, offering the ultimate and unaltered guidance from Allah. This view underscores the Quran's role in confirming and clarifying the messages of earlier scriptures, including the Injil, and highlights the continuity and consistency of divine guidance throughout history.
